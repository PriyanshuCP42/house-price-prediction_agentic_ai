# Housing Market Trends — King County, Washington

## Historical Context: 2014-2015 Market

The King County housing dataset covers sales from May 2014 through May 2015, a period characterized by:

- **Post-2008 Recovery:** By 2014, the Seattle-area housing market had largely recovered from the 2008 financial crisis. Prices were rising steadily, driven by tech sector growth.
- **Tech Boom Beginning:** Amazon, Microsoft, and other tech companies were expanding rapidly, attracting high-income workers and driving demand, particularly in Seattle, Bellevue, and Kirkland.
- **Inventory Constraints:** Housing supply was tightening as new construction lagged behind population growth. This seller's market pushed prices upward, particularly in desirable school districts and transit-accessible locations.
- **Interest Rates:** Mortgage rates in 2014-2015 averaged around 3.8-4.2% for 30-year fixed loans, historically low and supporting higher purchasing power.

## Geographic Price Patterns

King County exhibits strong geographic price gradients:

- **Eastside Premium:** Bellevue, Medina, Mercer Island, and Kirkland command the highest per-square-foot prices. Tech wealth concentration and excellent schools drive values.
- **Seattle Urban Core:** Capitol Hill, Queen Anne, and Wallingford show high PPSF due to walkability and urban amenities, though lot sizes are smaller.
- **North King County:** Shoreline, Bothell, and Kenmore offer moderate pricing with good schools and suburban character.
- **South King County:** Kent, Auburn, Federal Way, and Renton historically show lower prices but stronger value opportunities. These areas have seen significant appreciation since 2015.

## Waterfront Premium

Waterfront properties in King County command a significant premium. Properties with waterfront access typically sell for 60-200% above non-waterfront comparable properties in the same zipcode. Lake Washington, Puget Sound, and Lake Sammamish waterfronts are the most valued.

## Seasonal Sales Patterns

Real estate in King County follows typical Pacific Northwest seasonal patterns:
- **Spring (March-May):** Peak listing season. Highest inventory and transaction volume.
- **Summer (June-August):** Strong sales continue. Families prefer moving before school year.
- **Fall (September-November):** Slower market. Buyers may find better deals.
- **Winter (December-February):** Lowest activity. Serious sellers may accept lower offers.

## Important Data Limitations

When using a model trained on 2014-2015 data for property analysis, consider:

1. **Market Appreciation:** King County home prices have appreciated significantly since 2015. The model's absolute price predictions reflect 2014-2015 price levels, not current values.
2. **Relative Insights Remain Valid:** While absolute prices differ, the model's insights about which features drive value (location, size, grade) remain directionally accurate.
3. **Neighborhood Evolution:** Some areas have gentrified or declined since 2015. Zipcode-level comparisons may not reflect current neighborhood character.
4. **Infrastructure Changes:** New transit lines (Link Light Rail extensions), commercial developments, and zoning changes can significantly alter local property values.
5. **COVID-19 Impact:** The pandemic reshaped housing preferences (remote work, space requirements) in ways not captured by pre-2020 data.

This model should be used as an analytical framework for understanding price drivers and relative value, not as a current market price predictor.
